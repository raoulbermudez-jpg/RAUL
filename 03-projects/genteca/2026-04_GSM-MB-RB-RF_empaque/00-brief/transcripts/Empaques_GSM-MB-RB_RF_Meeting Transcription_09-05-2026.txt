- **Simon Sinek** — Start with the “why” and communicate the consumer benefit first, especially for packaging aimed at non-technical buyers.

## 🧠 Frameworks To Apply

- **Positioning Statement Framework** — Define who the product is for, what problem it solves, and why it is different.
- **Brand Architecture (Aaker/Keller)** — Separate master brand, product line names, and technology names to avoid clutter and strengthen protection.
- **Jobs-To-Be-Done** — Translate technical features into the job the product performs for the buyer, installer, or end user.
- **Message Hierarchy** — Front panel: one core promise; back panel: technical explanation; QR code: deep-dive content.
- **Naming Criteria Matrix** — Score candidate names by registrability, memorability, technical descriptiveness, market appeal, and fit with the brand.
- **Consumer vs. Technician Messaging Split** — Use different language for mass-market products and technical/installer-facing products.

## 🚀 Recommended Best Practices

- Keep the **front-of-pack message short** and benefit-led.
- Use **distinctive fantasy names** for technologies when possible; avoid purely descriptive phrases.
- Maintain **separate messaging tracks** for technical users and mass consumers.
- Validate names with **legal/IP review early** to reduce registration risk.
- Use **QR codes** to host detailed instructions, videos, diagrams, and expandable explanations.
- Present **one primary option plus backups** to avoid decision paralysis.
- Build a **competitor comparison** that shows how rivals name and explain similar technologies.
- Ensure the naming strategy supports both **protection against copycats** and **stronger market differentiation**.
- Test the proposed wording with **sales teams and technical users** before final packaging approval.
